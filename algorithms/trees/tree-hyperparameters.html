Tree Hyperparameters

Decision trees have several levers that directly control complexity — from max_depth (how tall the tree grows) to min_samples_leaf (how many samples must sit at each leaf). Getting these right is the difference between a tree that memorises noise and one that generalises beautifully.

⏱ 18 min ◆ Beginner

⚙
Every parameter and what it controls

Each hyperparameter controls a different aspect of tree complexity. Low values = simpler tree (risk of underfitting). High values = complex tree (risk of overfitting).

max_depth
How tall can the tree grow?
⬇ Low (1-3): shallow tree, high bias, fast, easy to interpret
⬆ High / None: deep tree, can memorise every training point
min_samples_split
Min samples to attempt a split
⬇ Low (2): tries to split even tiny groups — overfits
⬆ High (20+): only splits larger groups — simpler tree
min_samples_leaf
Min samples at each leaf
⬇ Low (1): allows single-sample leaves — severe overfit
⬆ High (10+): forces larger leaves — smoother boundaries
max_features
Features considered per split
⬇ Low / sqrt(n): random subset adds variance reduction
⬆ None / all: considers every feature at every split
criterion
Split quality measure
gini: faster, slightly prefers larger classes
entropy: info-theoretic, marginally smoother splits
max_leaf_nodes
Total number of leaves allowed
⬇ Low (5-10): very compact tree, easy to visualise
⬆ None: no limit on leaves — grows until other constraints stop it

max_depth — the most important parameter

max_depth is almost always the first parameter to tune. It directly controls how many decisions the tree can chain together. A tree of depth 1 makes a single split (a decision stump). A tree of depth 20 can learn extremely complex patterns — but memorises noise on small datasets.

Rule of thumb: Start with max_depth=3 or 4. If your training accuracy is too low, increase it. If your test accuracy is much lower than training, decrease it or increase min_samples_leaf. Most real-world trees work well between depth 3 and 8.

✂
Cost-complexity pruning — ccp_alpha

Beyond the growth-prevention parameters, sklearn also supports post-pruning via ccp_alpha (cost-complexity pruning alpha). After the tree is fully grown, branches that don't improve accuracy by at least alpha are removed. This often finds a better tree than stopping growth early.

Key takeaways

Summary
max_depth is the most important. Start at 3-5. Too deep = overfit. Too shallow = underfit.
min_samples_leaf prevents tiny leaves — the most effective regularisation after max_depth.
Use ccp_alpha (post-pruning) to fine-tune after growing a full tree. Find the best alpha with cross-validation.
Always compare train vs test accuracy. A large gap means overfitting. Both low means underfitting.
