K-Means Clustering

No labels, no teacher. K-Means finds natural groups in your data by repeatedly asking: "which cluster centre am I closest to?" It is one of the most used algorithms in all of machine learning.

⌛ 20 min ◆ Beginner numpy · sklearn

🌐
Intuition — sorting marbles into groups

Imagine you have 100 marbles scattered on a table and you want to sort them into 3 groups by colour. You don't have labels — you just look at them. K-Means does exactly this, but for any kind of data.

The algorithm is beautifully simple. It repeats just two steps until nothing changes:

  1. Assign — for every point, find which cluster centre (centroid) it is closest to. Assign the point to that cluster.
  2. Update — move each centroid to the average position of all points currently assigned to it.
💡

Key difference from supervised learning: K-Means has no labels. You do not tell it "this point is category A". It discovers the groups entirely on its own by finding points that are close together. This is called unsupervised learning.

How to choose K — the Elbow Method

K-Means needs you to say how many clusters you want upfront. But how do you know the right number? The Elbow Method tries K = 1, 2, 3, ... and plots the inertia (total distance of points from their centroid). The "elbow" in the curve — where adding more K stops helping much — is the best K.

Elbow method — inertia vs K
The elbow (sharp bend) points to the best K. Past the elbow, adding clusters gives little improvement.
Silhouette score — another way to choose K
Silhouette score ranges from -1 to 1. Higher is better. The K with the highest score is best.

When K-Means fails — know its limitations

K-Means makes assumptions about cluster shape. It works perfectly for round blob-shaped clusters. It completely fails on ring-shaped or crescent-shaped clusters. See for yourself:

∑
The algorithm — two steps repeated

1
Initialise — place K centroids
Pick K starting centroid positions. Random init picks random data points. K-Means++ picks spread-out starting positions (much better, this is the default in sklearn).
Random init: pick K random points from data as centroids KMeans++ init: 1. pick first centroid randomly 2. for each remaining centroid: pick a point with probability proportional to distance^2 from nearest existing centroid This spreads centroids out, avoiding bad starts
2
Assign — each point joins nearest centroid
Calculate the Euclidean distance from every point to every centroid. Assign each point to the cluster whose centroid is closest.
For each point x_i: cluster(x_i) = argmin_k ||x_i - centroid_k||^2 This is just: which centroid am I closest to?
3
Update — move each centroid to the mean
For each cluster, compute the average (mean) position of all points currently in that cluster. Move the centroid to that mean position. This is why it is called K-MEANS.
For each cluster k: centroid_k = mean of all x_i where cluster(x_i) == k = (1/|C_k|) * sum of all x_i in cluster k
4
Repeat until convergence
Keep alternating assign and update until the centroids stop moving (or move less than a tiny threshold). The algorithm is guaranteed to converge, but may find a local optimum, not the global best.
Objective: minimise Inertia (WCSS = Within-Cluster Sum of Squares) Inertia = sum over all points: ||x_i - centroid of its cluster||^2 K-Means always decreases inertia each iteration. It stops when inertia change is below tolerance (default 1e-4).

Common mistakes

✕
Not scaling features. K-Means uses Euclidean distance. A feature measured in thousands dominates features measured in units. Always use StandardScaler before K-Means.
✕
Assuming clusters must be round. K-Means only finds round (convex) clusters. For crescent, ring or irregular shapes, use DBSCAN or hierarchical clustering instead.
✕
Running K-Means only once. Results depend on random initialisation. Always set n_init=10 or higher to run multiple times and pick the best result. sklearn does this by default.
✕
Picking K by guessing. Always use the Elbow Method and Silhouette Score to guide your K selection. Also consider domain knowledge — what makes sense in your problem?
✕
Thinking cluster labels have meaning. K-Means labels are arbitrary. Cluster 0 today might be cluster 2 next time you run. Labels are only meaningful relative to each other, not in absolute terms.

★
Key takeaways

Summary
K-Means alternates between assign (each point joins nearest centroid) and update (move centroid to mean of its cluster) until convergence.
K-Means minimises inertia (total distance of points to their centroid). Use the Elbow Method or Silhouette Score to find the best K.
Always scale features first. K-Means uses Euclidean distance, so feature scales matter enormously.
K-Means only finds round, convex clusters. For irregular shapes, use DBSCAN or hierarchical clustering.
Use init='k-means++' (default) and n_init=10 (default) for reliable results across random starts.
