Hierarchical Clustering

Instead of picking K upfront, hierarchical clustering builds a full tree of merges — showing every level of grouping at once. You get to decide how many clusters you want after seeing the tree, by making a horizontal cut.

⌛ 20 min ◆ Beginner–Intermediate scipy · sklearn

🏠
Intuition — building a family tree

Imagine sorting animals into groups. You start with every animal as its own group (100 animals = 100 groups). Then you find the two most similar animals and merge them. Then the next two most similar groups merge. Keep going until everything is one big group. The result is a dendrogram — a tree diagram showing the full merge history.

The big advantage over K-Means: you don't need to pick K beforehand. You look at the dendrogram and draw a horizontal line at any height. Wherever that line crosses the tree, that is how many clusters you get.

💡

Two directions: Agglomerative (bottom-up) starts with every point alone and merges upward. Divisive (top-down) starts with one big group and splits downward. Agglomerative is far more common and is what scipy and sklearn implement by default.

Linkage methods — how do we measure cluster distance?

When merging two clusters, we need to measure the "distance" between them. But a cluster is a group of points — not a single point. Different linkage methods answer this differently, and they give very different trees.

Single
min distance
Distance = closest pair of points (one from each cluster). Creates long chain-like clusters.
Complete
max distance
Distance = furthest pair. Creates compact, equally sized clusters.
Average (UPGMA)
mean distance
Distance = average of all pairwise distances. Good compromise.
Ward
min variance
Minimises total within-cluster variance. Best for compact clusters. Most popular.
Same data — four different linkage methods
Ward linkage usually gives the most balanced and compact clusters.

How it works — agglomerative algorithm

1
Start: every point is its own cluster
With n points, you begin with n clusters each containing exactly one point. Compute the pairwise distance matrix — all n*(n-1)/2 distances.
Initial: n clusters = {p1}, {p2}, {p3}, ..., {pn} Distance matrix D: D[i][j] = distance(point_i, point_j) Shape: (n x n), symmetric
2
Find and merge the two closest clusters
Find the pair of clusters (i, j) with the smallest distance. Merge them into one new cluster. Record this merge in the dendrogram at height = their distance.
Find: (i, j) = argmin D[i][j] over all cluster pairs Merge: cluster_i and cluster_j become cluster_new Record in dendrogram: (cluster_i, cluster_j, merge_height)
3
Update distances with chosen linkage
After merging, update the distance from the new cluster to all remaining clusters. This is where linkage method matters — single, complete, average, and ward all compute this differently.
Single linkage: D(new, k) = min( D(i,k), D(j,k) ) Complete linkage: D(new, k) = max( D(i,k), D(j,k) ) Average linkage: D(new, k) = mean of all pairwise distances Ward linkage: D(new, k) minimises increase in total within-cluster variance
4
Repeat until one cluster remains
Keep finding and merging the closest pair until all points are in one cluster. The full dendrogram is now complete. You can then cut it at any height to get k clusters.
After n-1 merges: all points are in 1 cluster To get k clusters: draw a horizontal line in the dendrogram at height h such that it crosses exactly k branches scipy: fcluster(linkage_matrix, t=h, criterion='distance') sklearn: AgglomerativeClustering(n_clusters=k, linkage='ward')

Common mistakes

✕
Using hierarchical clustering on large datasets. Hierarchical clustering is O(n² log n) in time and O(n²) in memory. It becomes very slow and memory-heavy past ~10,000 points. For large data, use K-Means or DBSCAN instead.
✕
Picking the wrong linkage method. Single linkage is great for chains but bad for compact clusters. Ward is best for most real-world data. Complete and average are middle grounds. Always try Ward first.
✕
Not scaling features. Like K-Means, hierarchical clustering uses distance. Features with large scales dominate. Always use StandardScaler before fitting.
✕
Ignoring the dendrogram structure. The dendrogram tells you much more than just the number of clusters. Look at the heights — long vertical lines mean clearly separated clusters. Short branches everywhere means the clusters blend together without a natural split.

★
Key takeaways

Summary
Hierarchical clustering builds a full merge tree. You do not need to choose K upfront — cut the dendrogram at any height to get any number of clusters.
The linkage method defines how inter-cluster distance is measured. Ward (minimises variance increase) gives the most compact clusters and is usually the best default.
Look for long vertical gaps in the dendrogram — that is where the best cut is. A long gap means a natural, clear separation between clusters.
Slow on large data — O(n²). Use K-Means or DBSCAN for more than ~10,000 points.
Unlike K-Means, single linkage can find non-spherical clusters (chains, curves). Ward is better for compact blob clusters.
