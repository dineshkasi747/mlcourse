Outlier Detection: IQR Method

The IQR (Interquartile Range) method finds outliers using the middle 50% of your data as a reference — no mean, no standard deviation, no normality assumption. It's the rule behind every box plot: anything beyond 1.5 × IQR from the box edges is a suspected outlier.

⏱ 20 min ◆ Beginner numpy · pandas · scipy

□
How IQR works — the box plot rule

Sort your data. The middle value is the median. The middle 50% of the data — the range from the 25th to the 75th percentile — is the IQR (Interquartile Range). Multiply the IQR by 1.5 and extend that distance below Q1 and above Q3: anything outside those fences is flagged as an outlier.

The critical advantage over Z-score: the IQR is computed entirely from the middle of the data. Outliers cannot inflate it — making IQR naturally robust to the very values it's trying to detect.

Why k=1.5? John Tukey chose 1.5 because for a normal distribution it flags ≈0.7% of data — comparable to a Z-score of ±2.7. Use k=3 for "extreme" outliers only. The key insight: the IQR boundaries do not move when you add outliers, unlike Z-score boundaries which shift with every extreme value.

IQR vs Z-score on skewed data

Z-score assumes normality — on income, house prices, or any right-skewed distribution, the mean and std are distorted and the Z-score boundaries are unreliable. IQR uses percentiles which work on any distribution shape. This demo makes that difference visible.

The IQR formula — step by step

1
Compute Q1, Q3, and IQR
Sort the column. Q1 is the 25th percentile (lower quartile), Q3 is the 75th percentile (upper quartile). IQR is simply their difference — the width of the middle 50% of the data.
Q1 = percentile(X, 25) Q3 = percentile(X, 75) IQR = Q3 - Q1 # width of the middle 50%
2
Compute the fences
Multiply IQR by k (standard: 1.5). Subtract from Q1 for the lower fence; add to Q3 for the upper fence. Everything outside these fences is flagged.
lower_fence = Q1 - k * IQR (k=1.5 standard, k=3 extreme) upper_fence = Q3 + k * IQR flag if: x < lower_fence OR x > upper_fence
3
Why k=1.5 works for normal distributions
For a normal distribution, Q1 ≈ μ−0.6745σ and Q3 ≈ μ+0.6745σ, so IQR ≈ 1.349σ. At k=1.5, the fences sit at μ ± 2.698σ — flagging roughly 0.7% of a normal distribution, comparable to Z=2.7.
For Normal(μ, σ): IQR ≈ 1.349 * σ lower_fence ≈ μ - 1.5 * 1.349σ = μ - 2.698σ upper_fence ≈ μ + 2.698σ → flags ≈ 0.7% of data (similar to |z| > 2.7)
4
Fit on training data — apply to train and test
Just like any other statistic, Q1 and Q3 must be computed on training data only. Using test data to determine fences is data leakage. Wrap in a custom transformer inside a Pipeline.
Q1_train, Q3_train = np.percentile(X_train, [25, 75]) IQR_train = Q3_train - Q1_train lo = Q1_train - 1.5 * IQR_train hi = Q3_train + 1.5 * IQR_train mask_train = (X_train >= lo) & (X_train <= hi) # keep inliers mask_test = (X_test >= lo) & (X_test <= hi) # apply same bounds

Common mistakes

✕
Computing Q1/Q3 on the full dataset before splitting. The fences must be fit on training data only. Fitting on all data means test outliers influence the boundary — leakage. Always split first, then compute IQR statistics from X_train.
✕
Applying IQR removal inside a Pipeline that changes row count. sklearn Pipelines expect consistent n_samples between fit and transform. A row-dropping IQR step breaks the pipeline. Solution: apply IQR removal to training data before fitting the pipeline, then use the pipeline only on the cleaned data.
✕
Using k=1.5 blindly on all columns. k=1.5 flags ≈0.7% of a normal column — but on a truly heavy-tailed column it will flag 5–15%. Inspect the box plot and adjust k per column, or use k=3 for a more conservative approach.
✕
Dropping outliers before understanding them. A flagged value might be the highest-value customer, a record-breaking sensor event, or a genuine business extreme. Always investigate outliers — drop only confirmed data-entry errors. For real extremes, consider winsorization instead.
✕
Applying IQR to target variable y in regression. Removing extreme y values can introduce systematic bias — your model's training range no longer covers the true data range. Only apply outlier removal to features, not the response variable, unless you have a very specific justification.

★
Key takeaways

Summary
IQR detects outliers using fences at Q1 − k×IQR and Q3 + k×IQR. Q1 and Q3 are not influenced by extremes, making IQR inherently robust — the "masking problem" of Z-score does not apply.
k=1.5 (Tukey's standard) for mild outliers. k=3 for extreme outliers only. Tune k on a per-column basis when distributions differ significantly.
IQR is the preferred method for skewed distributions (income, prices, durations) where Z-score would produce many false positives on the long tail.
Always fit on training data — compute Q1/Q3 from X_train and apply the same fences to X_test. Wrap in a custom sklearn transformer for Pipeline safety.
IQR is a per-column method — it misses multivariate outliers (unusual combinations of two features that are individually normal). Use Isolation Forest or Mahalanobis distance for joint outlier detection.
Flagged values require human investigation before removal. Use df[outlier_mask] to inspect them — decide whether to drop, cap (winsorize), or keep each one based on domain knowledge.
